## B.  FUTURE OUTLOOK

## Global Economy

FY24 will be remembered as a year of resilience, with growth, inflation, and the labour market proving more robust than expected. Global growth surpassed forecasts mainly due to strong growth in the US. Robust government spending, buoyant services sector, a strong job market and healthy consumption aided US growth. Europe experienced slower growth due to elevated interest rates and energy prices. China faced challenges from its real estate sector and softening exports but growth remained supported through recovery in consumption and industrial  investment.  Commodity prices corrected due to disappointment in China's growth however, oil prices rose due to heightened geopolitical tensions and continuing production cuts by OPEC+. Inflation receded in most advanced economies (AEs) but remained at elevated levels, prompting central banks to maintain tight monetary policies, though most signalled the end of rate hikes cycle by end of FY24.

## Indian economy

Growth: India's growth momentum accelerated, with GDP growth (FY24: 8.2%, FY23: 7.0%) beating estimates on the back of robust investments led by real estate sector and government. Subdued rural income due to uneven monsoon along with elevated inflation, weighted on the private consumption growth. GVA growth also improved (FY24: 7.2%, FY23: 6.7%) driven by manufacturing and construction, despite a deceleration in services sector activity.

## Outlook:

Global growth is expected to be strong in CY24 at 3.2% YoY (Source: IMF, CY23: 3.1%) led by healthy growth in the US and stable Chinese economy. India's growth momentum remained robust, supported by buoyant economic activity, a buoyant residential market, government focus on capital expenditure, and strong corporate profitability. Growth prospects for India in FY25 remain bright driven by potential rural sector recovery, healthy investment activity continuing and easing inflation. While the external sector faces modest vulnerability due to elevated global interest rates, it is safeguarded by ample foreign exchange reserves and a manageable current account. Key risks include geopolitical tensions, inflation resurgence, central bank tightening further, and surge in energy prices.

## Equity markets

India became the 5th largest equity market globally and total market capitalization surpassed USD 4 trillion in FY24 driven by broad-based equity rally. NIFTY/BSE Sensex ended the year higher by ~29%/25% respectively. Midcap and Smallcap indices  significantly  outperformed.  Most  sectors  ended  the  year  with  strong gains with Utilities, Capital Goods, Auto, Healthcare, and Oil &amp; Gas being the key outperformers. Globally, most equity indices across Advanced Economies (AEs) and Emerging Markets (EMs) ended the year with gains, except for China and Hong Kong.

The strong performance of equity was driven by strong buying by Foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) as well as Domestic Institutional Investors (DIIs). In FY24, FPIs bought equities worth USD 25.3 billion (FY23: net seller of USD 5.1 billion). Further, DIIs purchased equities worth USD 25.2 billion (FY23: net purchase of USD 32.2 billion). Retail participation in the equity markets - directly or through mutual funds - rose in FY24. Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) flows, a proxy of retail participation in mutual funds, nearly doubled in FY24 from FY21 (USD 24 billion vs USD 13 billion) and kept the DII buying strong.

Outlook: As on March 31, 2024, NIFTY 50 was trading at ~20.7x FY25E price to earnings multiple. In addition, Market cap-to-GDP stood at ~105% (based on CY25 GDP estimates) and the gap between 10Y G-sec yield and 1Y-Forward NIFTY 50 earnings yield* remained at elevated levels [*Earnings yield = 1/(one year forward P/E)]. Thus, current valuation multiples are at premium to their historical averages. However, one should view these valuations in the context of healthy nominal GDP growth, a buoyant corporate earnings outlook and robust de-levered corporate and banking balance sheets.

We remain positive on equities over the medium-to-long-term considering the structurally robust domestic growth outlook, healthy corporate profitability and pro-growth policies. A sharp slowdown in global growth, escalation of geopolitical tensions and re-acceleration in inflation globally or locally are key near-term risks.

## Fixed Income markets

FY24 saw considerable volatility in global fixed income markets, especially in AEs, due to synchronized tightening by major central banks as both growth and inflation exceeded expectations especially in the US. Resilient economy in the US resulted in rate cut expectations being pushed out and US yields ended the year higher than last year. However, yields in Europe were relatively stable on back of weaker growth and rising expectations that ECB might cut rates before US Fed.

Indian G-sec yields declined, with 10-year Gsec yields ending at 7.06%, 25 basis points lower. In H1FY24, yields fell following in-line market borrowing in the budget and an unexpected RBI pause in April 2023. However, rising inflation coupled with liquidity turning into deficit and hawkish communication by RBI resulted in yields at the short end rising in H2FY24. The long-end yields, however, remained rangebound. India's inclusion in global bond indices boosted FPI flows post-September 2023(FY24: USD 14.2 billion, FY23: Net sale of USD 0.4 billion) and thus kept a lid on yield at the long end. Corporate bond spreads were largely stable throughout the year.

Outlook: Fixed income markets is favourably placed for FY25. The inclusion of  Gsecs in the JP Morgan bond indices is anticipated to bolster the demand outlook for G-Sec in FY25. Moreover, the government commitment to bring down the fiscal deficit to less than 4.5% of GDP by FY26, should help keep market borrowings at manageable levels. Significantly higher than expected dividend from RBI (INR 2.11 trillion against expectation of &lt;INR 1 trillion) should also provide government certain degree of freedom to achieve its fiscal target without sacrificing growth. Additionally, a comfortably placed external sector and ample foreign exchange reserves should keep pressure on INR at bay. Meanwhile, the subdued core CPI momentum should keep inflation under check. Moreover, as the rate hike cycle in AEs has ended, expectations of rate cuts from them are high in FY25. The RBI is also likely to cut rates in the second half of FY25, albeit we anticipate a shallow rate cut cycle. The key risks to the aforesaid favourable yield outlook are frequent food price shocks keeping inflation high and recent surge in commodity prices sustaining resulting in core CPI accelerating. In view of the above, yields are expected to trade with a downward bias and the long end of the yield curve is likely to outperform over the medium term.
